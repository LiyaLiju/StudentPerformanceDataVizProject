Overall Introduction

Our project analyzes how college students' daily habits may be influencing their academic performance and whether daily habits, like hours sleeping or attendance rate, differ based on major, the type of major, gender, or age. With this analysis, we want to discover trends that lead to student success and uncover any disparities between demographics so that those groups of students can receive targeted support. We also want to investigate whether the assumption that technical majors are more difficult than other majors is supported by real data.

To study the effects of different factors on college students' performance, we visualised comparisons between different categories of students and their habits. We mainly focused on comparing students by their major and gender and showing the individual statistics, such as hours spent studying, sleeping, and socializing, for those students. Another trend we focused on was visualizing the relationship between daily habits and academic performance, so we compared attendance rate and study hours with GPA change as well as sleep hours with GPA change.

Introduction to the Data

The dataset we analyzed contains information about individual college students, their demographics, and statistics about their daily habits and academic performance. The data contains 5000 rows and was collected from college students across different universities. You can access the original dataset here. We normalized weekly social hours to daily hours and added two new features: Major Type (Technical vs Non-Technical) and GPA Change (Final − Previous GPA).

Overview of Student Habits by Major

This chart contrasts the number of hours students spent studying, sleeping, and socializing each day to analyze if a student's major has an effect on their daily habits. From the visualization, the number of hours spent in each category is relatively equal for each major. Business majors have the most hours spent socializing while engineering majors have the least social hours. Business majors also get the most sleep per night at 7.07 hours on average. Engineering and psychology students have the least hours of sleep. Economics majors dedicate the most time to studying while mathematics majors spend the least time, though the difference is fractional. Grouping the data by major type reveals a similar trend with even statistics across majors. After comparing the three technical majors, engineering, computer science, and mathematics, with the three non-technical majors, psychology, economics, and business, the visualization shows that non-technical majors spend slightly more time in each category. This data suggests that college students dedicate the same amount of effort to their studies regardless of their major, and that the perceived difficulty of technical majors over non-technical majors is not reflected in the number of hours spent studying.

Correlation Between Habits and Performance

D3 Interactive — switch between grouping by major and age

This heatmap reveals that attendance rate and study hours have a strong positive correlation with Final CGPA across all majors and age groups, with attendance showing the strongest correlation ranging from +0.26 to +0.34. Sleep hours and social hours show very weak correlation, close to 0, meaning that these habits have little to no relationship with academic performance. This pattern is consistent across all majors and age groups. The key takeaway is consistent attendance and higher study time are the most important habits for achieving a high CGPA, while sleep and social hours do not significantly impact academic performance in this dataset.

Study Hours Per Day by Major

Altair

This chart displays the distribution of daily study hours across six majors to explore whether a student's field of study influences how much time they dedicate to studying. The interquartile ranges are very similar across all majors, with the middle 50% of students in every major studying between around 2.5 and 6 hours per day. The median study hours are nearly identical as well, suggesting that no single major stands out as significantly more or less demanding in terms of time spent studying. However, all majors show a notable number of outliers on the higher end, with some students studying up to 14 hours per day regardless of their major. Overall, the visualization indicates that daily study hours are mainly consistent across majors, and that individual student habits may play a larger role in determining study time than the major itself.

Study Hours Per Day vs GPA Change

Altair

The trend line shows a clear positive relationship: students who study more hours per day tend to see greater GPA improvement. Students studying less than 2 hours a day are roughly centered around zero or slightly below, suggesting minimal improvement or even a slight decline, while students studying 8 or more hours are almost entirely above the zero line. Despite the wide spread of points at every study level, the upward trend is consistent, indicating that study hours is a meaningful predictor of academic improvement regardless of major.

Summary

From the data, we can see that there was not a significant difference in how lifestyle or major differences affected student performance. Across all majors, students studied, slept, and socialized roughly the same amount, which led to similar results in how their GPA changed from semester to semester. There was also no correlation between technical majors and their daily habits differing from non-technical majors, which reveals no evidence to support technical majors being more challenging than non-technical ones. We found that regardless of major, higher attendance consistently led to better academic performance.

One notable observation was the gap in attendance rates between male and female Computer Science students, with female students attending about 2% less. With these findings, future work could investigate the causes behind this disparity and provide more equitable support to women in computing. Adding longitudinal data or tracking students across multiple semesters would also allow for a richer analysis of how habits evolve and compound over time.
